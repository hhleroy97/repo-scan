# Proximity Principle in Visual Design

## Summary

This source explains the Gestalt principle of proximity: elements placed close together are perceived as belonging to the same group, while whitespace separates unrelated items. It argues that proximity is one of the strongest grouping cues in UI design—often stronger than color or shape—and that thoughtful spacing improves scannability in navigation, text layout, and forms. It also warns that poor proximity (grouping unrelated controls or placing related actions far apart) causes users to miss important options, especially on mobile and responsive layouts where spacing relationships can break.

## Key claims

- Items close together are likely to be perceived as part of the same group with similar functionality or traits.
- Proximity can overpower competing visual cues such as similarity of color or shape.
- Using varying amounts of whitespace to unite or separate elements is key to communicating meaningful groupings.
- Related form fields grouped together make forms easier to scan and less intimidating to complete.
- Grouping unrelated elements together can camouflage them—users may judge an entire group based on one item.
- Elements separated by large whitespace appear unrelated and are easily overlooked by task-focused users ('tunnel vision').
- Responsive layouts can destroy proximity-based groupings when columns stack or spacing collapses, hiding related options.
- Headings and their associated content should be placed closer together than content from adjacent sections.
